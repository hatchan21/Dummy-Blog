Quantile Forecasting: Technology breakthrough brings > 20% higher inventory accuracy

In March, we announced the most significant technology upgrade since Lokad's launch: Quantile Forecast Technology Benchmarks against our classic forecasting technology with retail clients in food, non-food, hardware, luxury and spare parts show that quantile forecasts bring a performance improvement of over 20% - 30%, this translates to either 20-30% less inventory or 20-30% less stock outs.

In our opinion, quantile forecasts will make classic forecasts obsolete for inventory optimization in retail, wholesale and eCommerce. The technology is particularly powerful in 3 situations:

  • High service levels at 90% and above.
  • Intermittent demand (slow movers).
  • Bulk orders (spiky demand).

The development of Quantiles was triggered by the unsatisfactory results provided by classic forecasting technology (including ours) when applied to sparse data. Unfortunately a large part of product portfolios in commerce is slow moving ("long tail").

By applying the concept of quantiles to inventory optimization, we not only solve the problem caused by sparse data, but also significantly improved forecasting accuracy compared to classic methods.

The concept of quantile forecasting (also called quantile regression) has been known for decades in academic circles, and it has been applied successfully by financial analysts (Value at Risk analysis). We believe what has held the forecasting industry back has not been a lack of insights in statistics, but rather a lack of insights regarding in the profound relationship between quantiles and inventory optimization.

Big Data "really small": Processing a large retail network on a smartphone

Long before big data made technology headlines, retail networks have been among the pioneers in dealing with the large amounts of data that is produced by supply chain and and Point Of Sale. Recognizing the richness and immense value of their data, heavy IT infrastructure investments have been made.

However, to date, the limitations and cost of the required infrastructure has often left reality behind ambition and promise. This is particularly true for the richest retail data source: receipts generated by point of sale systems.

What about running a large retail network on a smartphone instead?

While this question is slightly provocative both from a technical and commercial point of view, we explain in this whitepaper how fundamental operations such as collecting and processing receipts for retail networks of up to 1000 stores can be done on a smartphone. The source code used by Lokad to produce the results exposed in this white paper has been made available as open source under a very liberal license (BSD) on GitHub.

How Big Data will transform Retail Marketing

Retailers and eCommerce today record large amounts of sales and client data, which provides a rich source of information for marketers.

However, the exploitation of this data to date has remained a rather costly, semi-manual and often high level exercise that is only scraping the surface of what promises to be a gold mine for marketing.

We believe Big Data technology will play an important role in the future of retail marketing by helping to address much more effectively the single most important goal of marketing: to provide the most relevant communication to individual clients at the right moment in time.

Our recent whitepaper examines how Big Data technology n the coming years will give marketers the tools to transform the effectiveness and ROI of their activities.

Big Data has set the stage for what we believe will be fundamental improvements in the way marketing can target, assess, optimize and ultimately convert campaigns into profits. Exciting times ahead!

Optimizing your Return on Inventory (ROI)

ROI of course usually stands for return on investment. However, the idea that every euro or dollar ‘invested’ in inventory brings a certain return in terms of profits is a powerful thought. When looking at inventory from this angle, two questions arise:

  • Are profits earned versus capital invested in stocking a product (i.e. ROI) similar over the product portfolio?
  • If the ROIs are heterogeneous, how can the overal ROI delivered by the inventory be maximized?

As you will have guessed, the answer to the first question is a decisive 'No'. Two aspects have a major impact.

First, the gross margin of a product directly affects the ROI. This is obvious, and product managers naturally try to build a portfolio with high margins in mind.

The second aspect is more subtle, but equally important: The amount of stock that is required to provide a desired availability (i.e. service level) varies significantly driven by the volatility of demand - the higher the uncertainty of future demand, the higher the inventory level required to assure a desired service level.

Businesses continually work on maximizing their return on capital employed (ROCE), and inventory is a significant part of capital. The good news is that the potential is significant. This is done by finding the optimal service level at which an SKU produces the best ROI within the constraint of a set inventory budget. As a result availability and revenue can be increased for a given inventory budget, or cash can be released from inventory while maintaining the overall availability.

The analysis behind this optimization however is complex given the non-linear correlation between service level and inventory levels. We have tackled this problem by including this optimization a priori in the forecasting and inventory optimization analysis. This brings us closer to optimizing directly what we ultimately need - the optimal return on inventory at the benefit of a much higher accuracy.
